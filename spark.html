Project Overview

A couples app that helps partners reconnect

Spark is a mobile app for iOS and Android that helps couples stay connected through playful, personalized coupons — small, thoughtful gestures that feel meaningful rather than obligatory.

I've always been fascinated by how design can shift emotional dynamics in overlooked spaces. As the Founding Product Designer, I owned the entire experience end-to-end: from the mobile app to sales and marketing dashboards, the marketing website, and a scalable design system that tied it all together.

Context

Busy schedules make
intimacy feel like a task

Couples want to keep their connection alive, but busy schedules, routines, and misaligned expectations can make intimacy feel like a chore instead of a joy. The moments that once came naturally get buried under notifications, work stress, and the monotony of daily routine.

Spark was born from the question: what if staying connected felt as easy and delightful as sending a meme?

The Problem

Most relationship-focused apps center around dating — not sustaining love. There's no app that makes it delightfully easy for committed couples to nurture their bond in the everyday.

Research & Discovery

Understanding how couples
stay connected today

I conducted qualitative research with couples across different relationship stages to understand their routines, friction points, and the role of small gestures in sustaining connection. The goal was to build empathy before building anything else.

Couples Interviews
In-depth conversations with couples at different relationship stages — dating, long-term, long-distance — to surface real patterns and pain points.
Competitive Analysis
Mapped the existing relationship app landscape — from LoveByte to Couple — to understand where intimacy apps succeed and where they fall short.
Love Languages Research
Studied how different love languages shape expectations around gestures — informing how Spark surfaces and personalizes coupon recommendations.
Surveys & Behavioral Data
Collected quantitative signal on frequency of gestures, willingness to plan dates, and what makes couples feel "seen" in their relationship.
User Testing (Beta)
Early prototypes tested with real couples — uncovering surprising insights about trust, vulnerability, and the emotional weight of sending vs. receiving a coupon.
Founder & Stakeholder Alignment
Weekly syncs with the founding team to align research findings with product vision, roadmap priorities, and technical constraints.

Key Insights

What couples actually need

Three themes emerged consistently across every research method — shaping every design decision that followed.

✦
Small gestures carry big emotional weight. A handwritten note or a spontaneous plan matters more than grand gestures. The frequency of small, intentional acts is the real predictor of relationship satisfaction.
✦
Planning feels unromantic, but structure enables spontaneity. Couples want things to feel effortless — yet they need light scaffolding to make gestures happen. The design challenge: make structure invisible.
✦
Vulnerability is the barrier, not effort. Many users said they knew what their partner wanted — they just felt awkward initiating. Spark had to lower the emotional activation energy, not just the logistical one.
How Might We

How might we make staying connected feel as natural and delightful as any other daily ritual — for couples who are already in love, but need a little nudge?

Ideation

Mapping every path
through the experience

Before any pixels, I mapped the full information architecture and user flows for all core interactions — coupon creation, redemption, settings, and the ambassador program. This ensured every edge case was accounted for and the development team had a single source of truth.

The core interaction had to be simple enough to use in 30 seconds, but rich enough to feel personal. Early wireframes went through 12+ rounds before we landed on the final architecture.

Turning users into advocates

1
Gamified level progression
Spark's Ambassador Program gamifies word-of-mouth growth. Users who share Spark with their friends earn real money — and progress through ambassador levels with escalating rewards. Level 1 & 2 ambassadors claim earnings via gift card email; Level 3+ unlock a special card revealing a physical gift.

Reflections

What I took away

01
Designing for intimacy requires balancing excitement with safety
Vulnerability is a core part of connection — and it means users are more cautious than in other product contexts. Every interaction had to earn emotional trust before it could ask for emotional investment. Delight is not enough; safety has to come first.
02
Simple, playful interactions create more delight than complexity
Early versions had more features — custom reminders, relationship quizzes, shared calendars. Testing showed that none of them mattered as much as making the core coupon flow feel magical. Restraint was the most powerful design decision I made on this project.
03
Love languages shape UX expectations in surprising ways
Users who give love through acts of service wanted structured redemption flows. Users who prefer words of affirmation wanted more copy customization. Understanding love languages as a design input — not just a marketing insight — fundamentally changed how we built the coupon composer.
